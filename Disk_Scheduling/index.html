Disk Scheduling

Disk Scheduling
Algorithms' Simulator

Disk scheduling is a technique used by operating systems to manage and optimize the order in which disk I/O (input/output) operations are performed on a hard disk drive or other storage devices. When multiple I/O requests are made by different processes or threads, disk scheduling determines the most efficient way to access and retrieve the requested data from the disk. The primary goal of disk scheduling algorithms is to minimize the overall disk seek time, which is the time required for the disk's read/write heads to move to the desired track or cylinder where the data is stored. By reordering the I/O requests intelligently, the operating system aims to reduce the amount of time wasted in seek operations and maximize the disk's throughput.

Simulations

FCFS

First Come First Serve

FCFS is the simplest disk scheduling algorithm. As the name suggests, this algorithm entertains requests in the order they arrive in the disk queue. The algorithm looks very fair and there is no starvation (all requests are serviced sequentially) but generally, it does not provide the fastest service.time. It operates on a queue-based approach, providing simplicity but potentially leading to longer waiting times for longer processes.

SCAN

SCAN

In SCAN disk scheduling algorithm, head starts from one end of the disk and moves towards the other end, servicing requests in between one by one and reach the other end. Then the direction of the head is reversed and the process continues as head continuously scan back and forth to access the disk. So, this algorithm works as an elevator and hence also known as the elevator algorithm.

CSCAN

CSCAN

The circular SCAN (C-SCAN) scheduling algorithm is a modified version of the SCAN disk scheduling algorithm that deals with the inefficiency of the SCAN algorithm by servicing the requests more uniformly. Like SCAN (Elevator Algorithm) C-SCAN moves the head from one end servicing all the requests to the other end.
